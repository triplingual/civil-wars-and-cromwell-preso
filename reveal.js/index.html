The English Civil Wars and Oliver Cromwell

A Presentation for SCSU HIS 589, Spring term 2015

Created by Trip Kirkpatrick / @triplingual

A Note on Terminology

I find it extremely interesting the sleight-of-hand with which this time of English history has been periodized. Even acknowledging the porosity of historical and geopolicial boundaries, a porosity that — to be fair — doesn't condense easily to the few words required of a textbook title, the time of civil wars, Commonwealth, Protectorate, and Convention disrupts a seamless notion of "Stuart England". My read is that Lockyer is a Royalist sympathizer (by which I mean he is anti-Cromwell). Then again, this is probably mostly just a case of history being written by the victors. In a rough parallel, here in the US, we describe our major war of the 1860s as being within a country rather than between two self-determined nations.

Overview

I'm not going to spend much time on foreign affairs.

I don't know about anyone else, but I found this all to be extraordinarily confusing. This presentation glosses over a number of significant details in the interest of taking an analytical step back.

I have some beefs with Lockyer's presentation of the information, but I have some sympathy with the difficulty in condensing all this information. I'll try to keep my frustration to a dull roar.

Chronology

Power and Freedom

a/k/a Nation-State

By which I mean the consensus of what sort of state England was during this time. The best way I found to figure this was to consider the layers and topics that I'm using in general to structure this presentation.

Last Years of Charles I

It seems fair to consider (as Lockyer implicitly does) England unchanged as a state from the onset of hostilities on August 22, 1642 to the execution of Charles I on January 30, 1649.

Commonwealth

At its longest, it lasted from the execution of Charles I in January of 1649 until the Restoration in 1660, considering the Commonwealth and the Protectorate not mutually exclusive, or, put another way, considering the Commonwealth to simply be England+Scotland+Ireland without a royal ruler.

At its shortest, it lasted from a Parliamentary declaration creating it on July 5, 1650 until August 4, 1654 when Cromwell dismisses the Rump Parliament

What was it? Who established it? Why? Who opposed it? Why?

Protectorate

Created when Cromwell dismisses the Rump Parliament and unilaterally accepts John Lambert's Instrument of Government, giving himself the top job of the Commonwealth (see how the Commonwealth didn't end?) of England, Scotland, and Ireland, ends when his son abdicates the title on May 7, 1659.

Restoration

Naturally, it wasn't quite that simple.

After Richard Cromwell abdicated, someone had to be in charge. At this time, it was the Council of Officers of the New Model Army (and Lockyer pretty much ignores that the NMA is still here and still taking names) running the show. They "invited" (Lockyer, 375) members of the Rump Parliament to re-form, which they did, sorta, meaning that the government became the Council of Officers plus a tiny Parliament, sufficiently composed of members of the original Rump Parliament that Lockyer calls is the Rump. Hijinks ensue when Parliament dismisses the head of the Council of Officers, which turns around and forcibly dismisses Parliament. Someone(?) forms a Committee of Safety (is the Council of Officers still in effect?), which takes charge of governing. The NMA cleaves, with part supporting the Committee of Safety and part backing Monck's aim of restoring the monarchy. Council of Officers sees it's cornered and dissolves the Committee of Safety ca. Christmas 1659, leaving the Rump Parliament in charge. Rump welcomes Monck, who invites Prides'-Purge-ed survivors back into Parliament. This reconstituted Long Parliament dissolves itself, and a Convention Parliament (or just a Convention) is elected. The Convention formally requests Charles II to return and proclaims him King of England. Exeunt.

Fighting

First Civil War

August 22, 1642 — May 5, 1646

Parliament: Long v1

State: Monarchy

3 years, 9 months of major hostilities, remembering that there was no fighting in the winter months. During this time, combatants may have simply returned home, depending on their ability to do so.

Second Civil War

May – August 1648

Parliament: Long v1

State: Monarchy

Parliament's supporters in Kent, Essex, Wales took up arms for Charles, so great was their discontent with Parliament. Key issues were soldiers' intolerable behavior (rape, theft, quartering) and excises that Parliament imposed to pay for war. Note that these were not the grand issues of the day, but rather quotidian concerns not unique to this period. However, they hadn't always resulted in popular armed uprisings.

Third Civil War

February 8, 1649 — September 3, 1651

Parliament: Rump v1

State: Commonwealth

Scots, angry that Parliament executed their king, proclaim Charles II king of Scotland, raise an army, and attack England/Parliament.

Themes

Fighting

What's an army? Who fights where for whom and for what? What are the national, regional, local boundaries of armed conflict and military obligation? What do soldiers fight for, and what do they get in return?

Before New Model Army

  • Who fights?
  • Why?
  • What's their reward? (What's punishment for not fighting?)
Lockyer asserts the answer to questions of motivation differs for commons soldiers and elites who commanded. Commoners fought, he claims, for "pay and the chance of plunder, as well as traditional loyalties". (342) I find this to be simplistic and dismissive, but Lockyer doesn't give enough information to build an informed counter-argument and I'm trying to stick here to Lockyer's text.

New Model Army

  • Who founds? Why?
  • Who fights? Why?
  • Does it achieve its goals? (Spoiler alert: Achieves Parliament's goals, then develops a life of its own.)
Who founds: Parliament, though technically it was the Committee of Both Kingdoms, a Parliament group who actually ran the war effort. Note that Parliament passed the Self-Denying Ordinance first, removing the offending nobility from commands. Note even more that Oliver Cromwell represented Cambridge as their MP, and never resigned that seat. Parliament made four exceptions to the SDO, including Cromwell. Why founded: This was serious business, but also before the point of no return, which turned out to be executing Charles I. Opinions of how to prosecute the war ranged, and two of those strongly suspected to prefer a peaceful solution commanded troops. As half-hearted commanders rarely win battles, Parliament wanted them gone, never to return. Who fights: Predominantly veterans of previous wars. Nominally, the NMA contained 22,000 men in cavalry and infantry. (I'm simplifying a bit, as this is not the most important part.) Many were impressed into service, and large numbers were puritans of particularly strong views -- Independents, who favored no central church at all. Does it achieve its goals: Hells yes, and then some. Goes rather farther than Parliament wants, perhaps. I continue to find new ways to be surprised at Lockyer's downplaying of the New Model Army. For instance, if you were from Sussex county and joined the New Model Army, you could conceivably (given my current limited knowledge) have traveled as far as Ireland and Scotland in your service. I'm likely rear-projecting too much, but that kind of expanded worldview changed political acting in the United States after World War I and World War II, so I'd be surprised if it didn't alter popular behavior of veterans in Early Modern England. By the same token, the state's construction restricted avenues of political participation differently, so I don't mean that behavior altered in the same way.

  • In any case, England (UK) returns to the militia model, with any training, recruitment, and activation in the hands of localities and their designated leaders. (377)

Equally interesting is that there had been popular interventions/uprisings before, but not with sufficient support to achieve anything. Spectrum I: National Church

Ultra Strong Weak None

Key document(s):

By 'Ultra' here, I mean Roman Catholic. That is, it's not merely a national church, it's an international one. But keep in mind that a strong position on a national church didn't necessarily mean a strong position on church governance. Put another way, some (Lockyer quotes one Sir John Holland on p349) considered any church where the power to remove someone from church membership to be a civil matter and some to be a clerical matter, but both of those groups could consider consistent wanting a national church. Also of importance: Lockyer remarks that independent churches arose and thrived as the country slid toward and into civil war. (349)

Spectrum II: Episcopacy

Ultra Strong Weak None

Key document(s): Covenant, Heads of the Proposals

Ultra position held by Catholics: Bishops answered only to Rome, could excommunicate Strong position held by C of E: Bishops answered to monarch, could excommunicate None position held by Independents: No bishops, leaving excommunication to individual congregations Weak position held by Presbyterians: Wide-area governance model (i.e. not complete independence of local churches) with a hierarchy and Knox's discipline but where church members select their representatives to governing councils.

Wait What

No, the Presbyterians didn't want bishops. But presbyterianism acknowledges and embraces a wide-area governance model, starting with presbyteries at the regional level, with a supra-church hierarchy that agrees upon acceptable practices and dogma for (and of) individual churches. This position occupied the middle ground on this issue during the Civil Wars, Commonwealth, and Protectorate, hence my placement and description. In addition, the body established by Parliament in the last months before the start of hostilities, the Westminster Assembly of Divines, revised the Book of Common Prayer.

Tolerance

Cromwell promoted religiously tolerance, and credited unity with diversity as the source of some of the Army's key victories. Yet he didn't extend his tolerance to, say, Irish Catholics, whom he and the New Model Army sent into slavery in Barbados by the thousands. Intolerance ran rampant. Case in point: Quaker James Nayler convicted of blasphemy, sentenced to be "branded, flogged, and have a hold bored through his tongue".

Levellers

Levellers enter the scene in 1647, wanted

  • Religious freedom
  • Increase in who could vote
  • Vulgar tongue in court
  • Trading monopoly restraint
  • Press freedom
Lockyer doesn't indicate how far the Levellers wanted for an "extension of the franchise". (353)

Levellers <3 Army

Well, quite a few Levellers were in the army. In any case, the Levellers and Army sympathisers published a joint document in 1647, The Case of the Army Truly Stated, combining Army military issues ith Levellers' social democratic concerns.

Making Flippy Floppy

I don't really see how someone involved with national politics at the time could have distinguished the situation from anarchy, or at least that's the impression Lockyer gives. Many entities switched sides or shifted active loyalties at one time or another. Welsh soldiers and Monck completely changed sides. Fairfax and other nobles, after ceding their commands, stayed out of the fight. Sir Henry Vane the Younger was the son of a Caroline minister, but stuck by the Roundhead cause the whole time.

Note how Lockyer has to use quote marks to disentangle nomenclature: Independents and "Independents", presbyterians and 'Presbyterians' (he uses single quotes, but he's using UK style)

In the End

England's not exactly back where they started, but there is some rough consensus among the voting population that

  • Standing armies are bad
  • Limited mon-archies aren't so bad
  • Stability is good

The common people, the vast majority of the population, did not go untouched by the events of this period, but you can't tell either way from Lockyer. Regular depredations during nine continual years of warfare and (it seems to me) nine more years of restless underpaid (or unpaid) soldiers could not help but have formed many peoples' outlook on the nature of the state and its relationship with the people. Concerning religion, though, I read between Lockyer's lines to claim that a culture of tolerance seems to have taken solid root, even as it existed side-by-side with the hegemony of a national church.
